Main Information about Coral Reef

Definition
What is a Coral Reef?

Coral reefs are intricate underwater ecosystems primarily composed of stony corals that form colonies of coral polyps, bound together by calcium carbonate. These reefs thrive in warm, shallow, clear, sunny, and agitated waters and have existed for approximately 485 million years, evolving from earlier microbial and sponge reefs. Belonging to the class Anthozoa within the phylum Cnidaria, corals are more resilient than their relatives, such as sea anemones, due to their ability to secrete hard exoskeletons for support and protection.

Often referred to as the "rainforests of the sea," coral reefs cover less than 0.1% of the world’s ocean area but support around 25% of all marine species, including fish, mollusks, crustaceans, and other marine invertebrates. These diverse organisms flourish in nutrient-poor waters, and while shallow tropical coral reefs are the most well-documented, deeper and colder water coral

reefs also exist, albeit on a smaller scale. The ecological importance of these reefs is profound, as they provide critical habitats for marine life and significantly contribute to the overall health of ocean ecosystems.

Despite their significance, coral reefs face numerous threats that jeopardize their existence, including:
Nutrient pollution from agricultural runoff
Rising ocean temperatures
Ocean acidification
Overfishing practices (such as blast fishing and cyanide fishing)
Pollution from sunscreen

Since 1950, shallow tropical coral reefs have experienced a dramatic decline of about 50%, primarily due to their vulnerability to changing environmental conditions.  

The economic value of coral reefs is substantial, offering essential ecosystem services that support tourism, fisheries, and coastal protection. Estimates of their global economic value vary widely, ranging from $30 billion to $375 billion based on older evaluations, up to $2.7 trillion in more recent assessments, and even reaching as high as $9.9 trillion. This highlights not only their ecological significance but also their critical role in supporting human economies and livelihoods. As coral reefs continue to face threats, their preservation is essential for maintaining biodiversity, supporting fisheries, and protecting coastlines from erosion. For more detailed information about coral reefs and their importance, resources from organizations such as the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) and the World Wildlife Fund (WWF) can be explored.

Types

Since Darwin's early classification of coral reefs into three main formations—fringing reefs, barrier reefs, and atolls—scientists have expanded our understanding to include additional types of coral reefs. According to Thomas, there are four major forms of large-scale coral reefs: fringing reefs, barrier reefs, atolls, and table reefs, as referenced from Stoddart's work (1969).  

In another study by Spalding et al., the classification includes four primary reef types: fringing reefs, barrier reefs, atolls, and "bank or platform reefs." They also note the existence of various other structures that do not fit neatly into these categories, such as "patch reefs," which can be found in different marine environments. This expanded classification reflects the diversity and complexity of coral reef ecosystems, highlighting how some reefs may not conform to traditional definitions but still play important ecological roles.

1 . Fringing Reef

A fringing reef, or shore reef, is directly attached to the shoreline or borders it with a narrow channel or lagoon. This is the most common type of coral reef, extending along coastlines and typically less than 100 meters wide, though some can be several hundred meters wide.
Fringing reefs are formed at low water levels and grow seaward, with their width determined by the seabed's steep drop-off. The reef surface generally stays just below the waterline. As older fringing reefs extend into the sea, erosion can deepen the inner part, forming lagoons that can exceed 100 meters in width and several meters in depth.

Directly attached to shorelines or located near them, often separated by a narrow, shallow lagoon.
The most common type of coral reef, these reefs develop along coastlines and can grow seaward as they expand.

2 . Platform Reef

Platform reefs, also known as bank or table reefs, grow on continental shelves or open ocean areas where the seabed rises near the surface, allowing coral growth. They are found in places like the southern Great Barrier Reef and the northern Mascarenes, sometimes several kilometers from the coast. Unlike fringing or barrier reefs that grow in one direction, platform reefs expand in all directions and vary greatly in size.
Platform reefs are often oval or elongated, with some parts reaching the water’s surface to form small islands or sandbanks. Over time, erosion can create lagoons or turn the reef into a pseudo-atoll, as seen in the Laccadives and near Jeddah in the Red Sea.

Form on continental shelves or open ocean locations where the seabed rises close to the surface, allowing coral growth.
Grow in all directions and vary widely in size and shape, with some forming small islands or sandbanks.

3 . Barrier Reef

Barrier reefs are separated from shorelines by deep channels or lagoons and are generally larger and formed in open water, unlike fringing reefs that grow directly along coastlines. Their lagoons can span several kilometers in width and reach depths of 30-70 meters. These reefs likely form as the seabed lowers or sea levels rise, making their development a slow process and resulting in their rarity.
The best-known example is the Great Barrier Reef in Australia , with other notable examples including the Mesoamerican and New Caledonian Barrier Reefs . Barrier reefs are also found in regions like the coasts of Providencia, Mayotte, the Gambier Islands , Kalimantan, Sulawesi, southeastern New Guinea, and the Louisiade Archipelago.

Separated from shorelines by a deep lagoon, which can be several kilometers wide and 30–70 meters deep.
Formed over a longer period than fringing reefs, barrier reefs grow offshore, with examples like the Great Barrier Reef in Australia.

4 . Atolls

Atolls, or atoll reefs, are circular or continuous reefs that completely surround a lagoon, usually without a central island. They typically form around volcanic islands as fringing reefs that, over time, erode and sink below sea level, leaving a ring-shaped reef around a lagoon. Atolls can also develop through seabed subsidence or rising sea levels.

These reefs are common in mid-ocean regions of the South Pacific, such as the Caroline Islands, Cook Islands, French Polynesia, Marshall Islands, and Micronesia. In the Indian Ocean, atolls are found in places like the Maldives, which includes 26 atolls—the Chagos Islands, Seychelles, and around Cocos Island.

Circular or continuous reefs that surround a central lagoon, typically formed from fringing reefs around volcanic islands that eventually submerge.
Commonly found in the South Pacific and Indian Ocean, with the Maldives being an example of an atoll-dominated region.

Other Types Beyond the Main Classifications
Apron Reef

Similar to a fringing reef but sloped downward from a point or shore, representing an early fringing reef stage.

Bank Reef

A larger, flat-topped reef generally found in mid-shelf areas, with a shape that is often linear or semi-circular. It is a type of platform reef.

Patch Reef

Isolated and smaller, patch reefs are found in lagoons or bays, often round and bordered by sand or seagrass. They can be part of fringing reefs, atolls, or barrier reefs.

Ribbon Reef

Long and narrow, these reefs are typically associated with the lagoons of atolls and are also called shelf-edge or sill reefs.

Drying Reef
This reef type is submerged at high tide but exposed at low tide.
Habili

Unique to the Red Sea, these reefs stay submerged, posing navigational hazards.

Microatoll

These coral communities are shaped by tidal limits, serving as low-resolution records of past sea levels through their fossilized structure

Cays

Low, sandy islands formed on reefs from sediment deposits, cays are often stabilized by vegetation and can support human habitation, seen across the Pacific, Atlantic, and Indian Oceans.

Seamount/Guyot

Created when a reef atop a volcanic island subsides; seamounts are rounded, while guyots are flattened by erosion, creating table-like structures called tablemounts.

Zones

Coral reef ecosystems contain distinct zones that host different kinds of habitats. Usually, three major zones are recognized: the fore reef, reef crest, and the back reef (frequently referred to as the reef lagoon).
The three zones are physically and ecologically interconnected. Reef life and oceanic processes create opportunities for the exchange of  seawater ,  sediments , nutrients and marine life.
Most coral reefs exist in waters less than 50 m deep. [56]  Some inhabit tropical continental shelves where cool, nutrient-rich  upwelling  does not occur, such as the  Great Barrier Reef . Others are found in the deep ocean surrounding islands or as atolls, such as in the  Maldives . The reefs surrounding islands form when islands subside into the ocean, and atolls form when an island subsides below the surface of the sea.

1 . Reef Surface

Shallowest part of the reef.
Subject to surge and tides.
Ideal conditions for coral growth due to sufficient light for photosynthesis and agitated water that brings plankton for feeding.

2 . Off-Reef Floor

Shallow sea floor surrounding a reef.
Typically found on continental shelves.
Usually sandy and may support seagrass meadows, which serve as foraging areas for reef fish.

3 . Reef Drop-Off

Extends for the first 50 meters from the reef.
Provides habitat for reef fish seeking shelter on the cliff face and plankton in nearby waters.
Primarily found around oceanic islands and atolls.

4 . Reef Face

Located above the reef floor or drop-off.
Often the most diverse area of the reef.
Complex habitats created by coral and calcareous algae, providing shelter in cracks and crevices.

5 . Reef Flat

Sandy-bottomed area that may be behind the main reef or between the reef and the shore.
May contain chunks of coral and can border a lagoon.
Fish often prefer this habitat when present.

6 . Reef Lagoon

Completely enclosed region, less affected by wave action.
Often contains small reef patches.
Provides a calmer environment for various marine life.

Locations

Most corals thrive in warm waters above 20 °C (68 °F) , which is crucial for their growth and survival. The boundary for the 20 °C isotherm indicates the regions where water temperatures are suitable for coral reefs to flourish. This boundary outlines a significant area of the world's oceans where conditions support coral ecosystems. In contrast, regions like the southwest coast of Africa and the coast of Peru experience cooler waters due to upwelling, where deep, colder water rises to the surface. This upwelling brings nutrients to the surface, promoting fish populations, but it also results in lower temperatures that are not conducive to coral growth. As a result, these areas have fewer coral reefs.

The map indicates regions of upwelling, highlighted in red, where colder, nutrient-rich waters rise to the surface. These areas are typically inhospitable to coral reefs because the cooler temperatures are not conducive to coral growth. Corals thrive in warmer waters, generally between 26°C and 28°C (79°F and 82°F). The nutrient-rich conditions in upwelling zones often favor the growth of phytoplankton and other organisms rather than coral. Consequently, coral reefs are generally absent in these coastal regions, as the environmental conditions do not support their development.
