Bunaken National Park
North Sulawesi, Indonesia

Bunaken National Park, located in northern Sulawesi, Indonesia, is a significant marine park that spans 890.65 km², with 97% of its area being marine habitat. Established in 1991, it is part of the Coral Triangle, hosting an impressive 390 species of coral along with diverse marine life, including fish, mollusks, and marine mammals. The park consists of five islands: Bunaken, Manado Tua, Mantehage, Nain, and Siladen, along with some coastal areas.

Geologically, the region is relatively young, shaped by volcanic activity over millions of years. The park features a distinctive vertical coral wall and various underwater ecosystems, with depths ranging from 200 to 1,840 meters. The coral ecosystem is rich, comprising about 390 species and supporting approximately 2,000 species of fish, including notable species like the emperor angelfish and spotted seahorse. On land, the islands are home to unique flora and fauna, including various palm species and animals like the Celebes crested macaque.

Human habitation within the park is significant, with around 35,000 people living in 22 villages. Most residents are involved in fishing or farming, while tourism has grown, offering accommodations from budget cottages to luxury resorts. Scuba diving and snorkeling are popular activities, making Bunaken a renowned destination for underwater enthusiasts   .

Mesoamerican Barrier Reef
Yucatán Peninsula to Honduras

The Mesoamerican Barrier Reef System (MBRS), also known as the Great Mayan Reef, extends over 1,126 kilometers (700 miles) along the coasts of Mexico, Belize, Guatemala, and Honduras. This reef system is the second-longest in the world and includes various protected areas like the Belize Barrier Reef, Arrecifes de Cozumel National Park, and the Sian Ka'an Biosphere Reserve. Approximately 30% of the MBRS is located off Belize’s coastline, showcasing its significance to the region's marine biodiversity.

The MBRS hosts over 65 species of stony coral, 350 species of mollusks, and more than 500 species of fish. It is a critical habitat for endangered species, including five types of sea turtles and the West Indian manatee. Notably, the northern regions near Isla Contoy are frequented by whale sharks, which are the largest fish in the world.

However, the MBRS faces severe threats, including hurricanes, rising sea temperatures, ocean acidification, pollution, overfishing, and invasive species like lionfish. The lionfish, native to the Indo-Pacific region, has become a significant problem as they prey on key reef species, leading to ecological imbalances and threatening commercial species such as lobsters.

Sha`b Abu Nuhas
Northern Red Sea

Shaab Abu Nuhas, located northwest of Shadwan Island in the northern Red Sea near Hurghada, is a prominent triangular coral reef known for its stunning underwater scenery and significant shipping history. The reef's location makes it a navigation hazard, as it extends into the busy shipping channel of the Red Sea. This hazardous positioning has led to numerous maritime accidents, with at least seven notable shipwrecks documented over the years.

Among these wrecks are the **SS Carnatic**, which sank in 1896, and more recent incidents like the **Kimon M** (1978), **Chrisoula K** (1981), and the **Giannis D** (1983). These wrecks, particularly the four located at depths greater than 30 meters (98 feet), attract divers from around the world who seek to explore their remnants and the diverse marine life that inhabits the area. The rich marine biodiversity, combined with the historical significance of the wrecks, makes Shaab Abu Nuhas a popular destination for scuba diving enthusiasts.

The reef's name translates to "reef of father of copper," a reference to the cargo of one of the shipwrecked vessels. The waters around Shaab Abu Nuhas are characterized by vibrant coral formations, colorful fish, and the remnants of the shipwrecks, creating a unique underwater ecosystem. This area is not only significant for recreational diving but also plays an essential role in marine conservation efforts, as the ecosystem's health is vital for sustaining local biodiversity.

Diving sites around Shaab Abu Nuhas offer various challenges and experiences, making them suitable for both novice and experienced divers. Some dives may involve navigating through narrow passages or exploring deep wrecks, providing opportunities to observe a wide range of marine species, including sharks, rays, and myriad fish species that thrive in the coral gardens.

Minami-Tori-shima
Japan

Minamitorishima, or Marcus Island, is a small coral atoll in the northwest Pacific Ocean, positioned over 1,800 kilometers southeast of Tokyo. As Japan's easternmost territory, Minamitorishima is notable for extending Japan's Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ), providing a vast area of potential marine resources. Although uninhabited by civilians, the island hosts a Japanese meteorological station and a Japan Coast Guard detachment.

The island's strategic importance lies in its location and its influence over a significant EEZ, which could allow access to valuable undersea minerals and other natural resources. Minamitorishima is also a key station for weather monitoring, playing an essential role in meteorological research that impacts weather forecasting across the Pacific region.

Geographically, Minamitorishima is isolated and exposed to the elements, which poses challenges for maintaining infrastructure. The island's limited vegetation and small size further highlight its remote and strategic nature, representing Japan's reach into the Pacific and underscoring its maritime interests.

Lyra Reef
Papua New Guinea

Lyra Reef is a coral reef located in the Coral Sea, approximately 24 kilometers from the northeastern coast of Australia. It is part of the larger Great Barrier Reef Marine Park, known for its rich biodiversity, including various coral species and marine life. The reef is also recognized for its unique underwater topography, which attracts a range of marine species.

This reef is significant not only for its ecological importance but also for its role in marine research and conservation efforts. It provides vital habitats for numerous species, including fish, mollusks, and crustaceans, contributing to the overall health of the marine ecosystem in the region. The area is monitored for environmental changes and threats, ensuring ongoing protection.

Lyra Reef is also a popular site for recreational activities such as snorkeling and diving, drawing tourists to experience its underwater beauty. This has raised awareness of the need for sustainable tourism practices to protect its fragile ecosystem. Efforts to maintain and restore the reef are ongoing, emphasizing the importance of preserving this natural treasure.

Tubbataha Reef
Sulu Sea, Philippines

The Tubbataha Natural Park, located in the heart of the Sulu Sea in the Philippines, is an extensive marine and bird sanctuary that encompasses two large atolls—North and South Atolls—and the smaller Jessie Beazley Reef, spanning a total area of 97,030 hectares (239,800 acres). Positioned 150 kilometers southeast of Puerto Princesa, Palawan, the park lies within the remote island municipality of Cagayancillo. This UNESCO World Heritage Site, designated in 1993, is recognized for its pristine coral reefs, rich biodiversity, and significant ecological role in marine conservation. The North Islet provides vital nesting grounds for both marine turtles and a variety of seabird species, and the entire reef system exemplifies a well-preserved coral ecosystem with dramatic underwater features, including a spectacular 100-meter vertical wall and extensive lagoons.

As part of the Coral Triangle, Tubbataha is situated in a global center of marine biodiversity that holds 75% of the world’s known coral species and 40% of the world's reef fish. This biodiversity hotspot contains no fewer than 600

fish species, 360 coral species, 11 species of sharks, 13 species of dolphins and whales, and approximately 100 bird species. The reefs serve as nesting grounds for endangered Hawksbill and Green sea turtles, further enhancing their ecological importance. Tubbataha faces ongoing threats from overfishing and destructive fishing practices, although its protected status and remote location have preserved much of its natural wealth. Since the 1980s, studies conducted by scientists have provided deeper insight into the reef’s significant marine diversity, underlining the park’s role in the conservation of coral reef ecosystems within the Philippines.

Geologically, Tubbataha Reef is situated on the Cagayan Ridge, which was once an active volcanic arc in the Miocene epoch. The reef system follows Darwin’s theory of atoll formation: it initially formed as a fringing reef surrounding volcanic islands, which then subsided as volcanic activity ceased, leaving the corals to grow and maintain their structure as an atoll. The gradual subsidence of the crust under the Cagayan Ridge, along with fluctuating sea levels over millennia, has sculpted the unique, terrace-like profile of the reef. The present geological makeup of Tubbataha, with its distinct, stair-stepped slopes, is a testament to the coral’s adaptability and the complex geological processes that have shaped the Sulu Sea region over millions of years.

Efforts to protect Tubbataha began in response to destructive fishing practices that became more prevalent in the 1980s. This led to a campaign by scuba divers and environmentalists, supported by the Palawan provincial government, resulting in Tubbataha’s designation as the Philippines’ first national marine park in 1988. The park was later expanded in 2006, encompassing Jessie Beazley Reef, and is vigilantly protected by armed rangers stationed in a fortified structure on the North Atoll. Rangers conduct 24/7 surveillance to guard against illegal fishing and poaching activities. In 2013, marking the park's 25th anniversary, the World Wildlife Fund launched plans for a modern ranger station to strengthen protective measures. Tubbataha’s isolation and vigilant protection efforts have preserved its marine ecosystems, making it a global symbol of marine conservation and biodiversity.

South Sentinel Island
Andaman Island

South Sentinel Island, located in the Bay of Bengal, is one of the Andaman Islands and measures approximately 1.6 kilometers long and up to 1 kilometer wide, encompassing an area of only 1.61 square kilometers. This small island, which is uninhabited, belongs to the Port Blair tehsil within the South Andaman administrative district, forming part of the Indian union territory of Andaman and Nicobar Islands. Positioned 59.6 kilometers south of North Sentinel Island, it lies 26.5 kilometers west-northwest of Little Andaman Island. Described as a forested coral reef, South Sentinel is surrounded by a bank that extends about 11 kilometers northwest, with considerable depths in the channel separating it from Little Andaman.

Although politically part of Little Andaman Tehsil, South Sentinel Island is primarily known for its uninhabited status, with occasional visits by diving expeditions drawn to its remote and untouched nature. In 2018, the Indian government relaxed the Restricted Area Permit regime for 29 islands, including South Sentinel, aiming to boost

tourism. However, this relaxation was intended mainly for researchers and anthropologists who could visit the island with prior approval, maintaining strict controls over access to the area due to its ecological significance and the need for conservation.

The South Sentinel Island Sanctuary, established in 1977, serves as an important habitat for various species, including the coconut crab, which has largely disappeared from other parts of the Andaman Islands. The island is particularly known for the massive nesting population of pied imperial pigeons, which return each March from South and Little Andaman to breed. Additionally, small numbers of Nicobar pigeons nest on the island, while the globally threatened Andaman crake can also be found here. South Sentinel is crucial for nesting green sea turtles, though their numbers have dwindled significantly due to past poaching activities. Leatherback turtles also nest on the island, and it is home to notable reptile species such as the Andaman water monitor and saltwater crocodile, alongside the endemic Andaman horseshoe bat, which is the only terrestrial mammal present on the island.

Overall, South Sentinel Island represents a unique ecological environment, offering a glimpse into the rich biodiversity of the Andaman Islands while also highlighting the challenges of conservation in a region impacted by human activity. The island's isolation, coupled with its diverse fauna, underscores the importance of protective measures to ensure the survival of its delicate ecosystems and the species that depend on them.

Qixingyan
Pingtung County, Taiwan

Qixingyan, also known as Ch'ihsingyen or the "Seven Star Reef," is a captivating group of coral islands situated in the Bashi Channel off the southern coast of Pingtung County, Taiwan. This unique island group comprises seven coral reefs that are fully visible at low tide, while only two reefs can be seen during high tide. The islands resemble the Big Dipper constellation, which inspired the name "Seven Star Reef." Located approximately 8 nautical miles (15 kilometers) from Eluanbi, the southernmost point of Taiwan, Qixingyan stands as a remarkable natural formation with significant ecological and geographical features.

The waters surrounding Qixingyan are infamous for their strong currents and treacherous shoals, which have historically posed challenges for navigation. Following the Qing court's opening of trade routes between the West and China in the early to mid-19th century, the area experienced an increase in maritime traffic, leading to numerous shipwrecks. Among the most notable tragedies was the sinking of the US merchant ship Rover in 1867, known as the Rover incident, which resulted in significant loss of life. Additionally, the 1871 wreck of the Japanese merchant vessel

Ryukyu, referred to as the Mudan incident, further illustrates the perils faced by ships navigating these dangerous waters.

Ecologically, Qixingyan plays a critical role in supporting marine biodiversity. The coral reefs serve as vital habitats for a variety of marine species, contributing to the overall health of the Bashi Channel ecosystem. Conservation efforts are crucial in protecting these reefs from the pressures of overfishing and tourism, ensuring the sustainability of the region's marine life. To this day, Qixingyan continues to stand as a beautiful and essential component of the marine landscape, reflecting both the natural splendor and ecological importance of the area.
