Apo Reef
Mindoro Strait, Philippines

Apo Reef, located in the western waters of Occidental Mindoro in the Philippines, is the world's second-largest contiguous coral reef system, spanning 34 km². It lies about 28 km west of the Mindoro coast and is protected under the Apo Reef Natural Park (ARNP), a marine protected area. The reef system is known for its rich marine biodiversity and is a popular diving destination. Apo Reef is triangular, with northern and southern atoll-like formations separated by a deep channel, ranging from 1.8 to 30 meters in depth. It is on the tentative list for UNESCO World Heritage status due to its ecological significance and is home to a variety of coral species and other marine life.

The reef is also recognized as an Important Bird Area (IBA) by BirdLife International, supporting a significant seabird population with at least 10,000 breeding pairs. Geologically, the reef has grown over an older formation from the last glacial cycle about 19,000 years ago, shaped by fluctuating sea levels. The reef’s northern lagoon is shallow, with depths of 2 to 10 meters, while the southern lagoon reaches up to 30 meters. The reef system plays a critical role in

sustaining marine life and preserving the region’s environmental balance.
Above the water, three islands—Apo Island, Apo Menor, and Cayos del Bajo—mark the reef’s surface. Apo Island, the largest, spans 22 hectares and features mangroves, white beaches, and karstic rock formations. It houses a lighthouse and a ranger station tasked with monitoring the reef, as the area has been designated a "no-take zone" since 2007 to ensure its protection. Apo Menor, a rocky limestone island, lies 3 km east of Apo Island and is largely uninhabited. The islands serve as critical components of the overall reef system, providing habitats and protection for various species while supporting conservation efforts.

Filippo Reef
450 km east of Starbuck Island in the Pacific Ocean

Filippo Reef is a reef that is asserted to be in the Pacific Ocean at 05°30′S 151°50′W, 450 kilometers (280 mi) east of Starbuck Island in the Line Islands. The reef was reported by the master of the Italian barque Filippo as having been seen on 28 June 1886. From an unidentified report of breakers dated 1926, it was estimated to have a water depth of only 0.6 to 0.9 meters (2 to 3 ft), and appeared to be about 1.6 kilometers (1.0 mi) long northwest to southeast, and less in width.

The only sighting referenced shows a date of 1926. The topographic data supplied by the General Bathymetric Chart of the Oceans (GEBCO), however, shows a sea depth of 3.3 miles (5.3 km).[3][4] It is therefore likely that this 1926 report is an error, and that Filippo Reef is a phantom island, and that therefore any maps showing it are relying on the 1926 report and are also in error.Nevertheless, it is marked in the 2015 edition of the National Geographic Atlas of the World.

Hawai Maro Reef
Maro Reef
Northwestern Hawaiian Islands

Maro Reef, known in Hawaiian as Nalukākala, meaning "surf that arrives in combers," is a unique and largely submerged coral atoll in the Northwestern Hawaiian Islands. Discovered in 1820 by Captain Joseph Allen aboard the ship *Maro*, the reef was named in honor of his vessel. Spanning a vast area of 747 square miles (1,935 km²), Maro Reef is the largest coral reef in the Northwestern Hawaiian Islands and a critical component of the Papahānaumokuākea Marine National Monument, one of the largest marine conservation areas in the world. What sets Maro Reef apart from most other atolls is its unusual structure. Instead of forming a continuous ring, the coral radiates outward from the center in a spoke-like pattern, creating a network of coral ridges and channels that resemble a wheel.

Maro Reef is located about 850 miles (1,370 km) northwest of Honolulu, Hawaii, and only a tiny portion of the reef, about 1 acre (4,000 m²), rises above the water, depending on tidal conditions. This landmass is often submerged

during high tide, making it a particularly vulnerable ecosystem. The reef's 37 species of stony coral are home to a variety of marine life, including fish, mollusks, and sea turtles. Despite its biodiversity, Maro Reef faces challenges from climate change and rising sea levels. Due to its fragmented coral structure, which is detached in some areas, scientists warn that Maro Reef may be "on the verge of drowning." Powerful storm waves pose a significant threat to the reef's integrity, as its fragmented nature leaves it especially susceptible to erosion and destruction.

The future of Maro Reef is closely tied to broader environmental changes, including global warming and ocean acidification, which impact coral health worldwide. In addition to its ecological importance, Maro Reef also plays a significant cultural role in Hawaiian heritage. As part of the Northwestern Hawaiian Islands, the area holds cultural significance for Native Hawaiians, who regard the region as a sacred place tied to ancestral spirits and cosmology. Conservation efforts within the Papahānaumokuākea Marine National Monument aim to protect both the ecological diversity and the cultural heritage of Maro Reef and surrounding areas. Despite these efforts, the reef’s future remains uncertain as it faces ongoing environmental threats.

Necker Island
Northwestern Hawaiian Islands

Necker Island, known as Mokumanamana in Hawaiian, is a small, rocky island located in the Northwestern Hawaiian Islands, approximately 430 miles northwest of Honolulu. Covering an area of about 45.193 acres, it features steep, eroded sides and very little soil, with its highest elevation reaching 277 feet. The island is significant for its prehistoric archaeological sites related to Hawaiian culture and is part of the Hawaiian Islands National Wildlife Refuge within the Papahānaumokuākea Marine National Monument.

Politically, Necker Island falls under the jurisdiction of the City and County of Honolulu, but it is managed by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, with no resident human population. The island is a remnant of a volcanic cone and is roughly 1.3 kilometers long, characterized by a hook shape. Notable geographical features include Mo'o Point, the westernmost point, and Northwest Cape, which juts northeast from the island and helps enclose Shark Bay on the northern shore. This bay is typically subject to rough seas. Additionally, a small islet lies just off the eastern tip of Necker Island. The island's rugged landscape and historical significance make it an important site within the Hawaiian archipelago.

Kingman Reef
Kingman Reef
North Pacific Ocean

Kingman Reef is a largely submerged atoll in the North Pacific Ocean, located between Hawaii and American Samoa. Spanning about 9 nautical miles (20 km) east-west and 4.5 nautical miles (8 km) north-south, it covers 3 hectares (0.03 km²) and is an unincorporated territory of the United States. Administered by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service as the Kingman Reef National Wildlife Refuge, it was claimed by the U.S. in 1859 and briefly served as a stopover for commercial flying boats in the 1930s. The Navy managed the reef from 1934 until 2000, when it transitioned to its current status as a marine protected area.

The reef features a deep lagoon, reaching depths of up to 53 fathoms (318 ft), and includes two small strips of dry land composed of coral rubble and giant clamshells. The highest point is less than 5 feet (1.5 m) above sea level, making it a maritime hazard. In the 21st century, Kingman Reef is recognized for its rich marine biodiversity, hosting hundreds of species of fish and coral. Its remote and protected nature allows it to serve as an important ecological site in the Pacific, while it remains free of economic resources or activities.

Yabiji Coral Reef
Yabiji
Ryukyu Islands

Yabiji, also known as Yaebiji or Providence Reef, is the largest coral reef group in Japan, located in the Miyako Islands of Okinawa Prefecture. Comprising over 100 coral reefs, Yabiji becomes visible several times a year, most notably around March 3 of the lunar calendar (early April in the Gregorian calendar), when low tides reveal parts of the reef. Due to its intermittent exposure during low tides, Yabiji is often referred to as the "Phantom Continent." In 2013, it was officially recognized as a Natural Monument of Japan, highlighting its environmental and cultural significance. Yabiji covers approximately 17 kilometers north to south and 6.5 kilometers east to west, with its Main Reef composed of eight large coral formations.

The reefs of Yabiji are uniquely named after various human body parts and animals, reflecting local traditions and culture. For instance, "Du" means torso, and "Budai" translates to parrotfish. The reef's structure aligns with the geological formations of the surrounding Miyako Islands, and it is believed that parts of Yabiji may have been dry land

during the Last Glacial Period. Today, the area remains mostly submerged, but during certain low tides, the reefs briefly emerge above the water. The name "Yabiji" is thought to come from either the eight main coral reefs or the layered structure of the reef group, with variations such as "Yapiji" used in some local areas.

Culturally, Yabiji holds significance for the Miyako Islands, particularly during the annual Sanitsu event, held on March 3 of the lunar calendar. This tradition involves women gathering to ward off bad luck as the reefs emerge during the largest tidal difference of the year. Yabiji’s rich marine biodiversity makes it a popular destination for freediving, scuba diving, snorkeling, and recreational fishing. The coral colonies provide an excellent fishing ground, attracting both locals and visitors. With its blend of natural beauty, cultural heritage, and ecological importance, Yabiji remains a key feature of the Miyako Islands' landscape.

Virgin Islands
Caribbean Sea

One of the most vital marine ecosystems in the U.S. Virgin Islands is the coral reefs, which span across the waters between St. Croix, St. Thomas, and St. John. These coral reefs cover an area of approximately 297.9 km² and are accompanied by various other marine habitats. Coral reefs develop through a natural process where free-swimming coral larvae attach to hard surfaces around the islands. These larvae then build external skeletons for protection, which also create surfaces for new coral to grow. Coral reefs in the Virgin Islands take on three distinct forms: fringing reefs, which grow close to the shore; barrier reefs, which are parallel to the shore but separated by deeper waters; and atoll reefs, which encircle lagoons or bodies of water.

The distribution of these reefs across the Virgin Islands varies. St. Croix is home to a well-developed barrier reef system along its eastern and southern shores, with key areas like Salt River Bay National Historical Park and Buck Island Reef National Monument protecting these ecosystems. Meanwhile, the coral reefs around St. Thomas and St.

John, including the Virgin Islands Coral Reef National Monument, are more patchy and scattered. Notably, fringing reefs, patch reefs, and spur-and-groove formations are found in these areas, providing vital habitats for diverse marine life.

The reefs are a hotspot of biodiversity, hosting more than 40 species of scleractinian corals and three species of Millepora (fire corals). A survey conducted from 2001 to 2006 documented 215 species of fish around St. John and 202 species around St. Croix. Additionally, the coral reefs are home to four species of sea turtles. These ecosystems are influenced by nutrient-rich anticyclonic rings originating from the Amazon and Orinoco Rivers, which bring freshwater nutrients that help sustain marine life. However, despite their richness and importance, these coral reefs are under threat from human activities, climate change, and other environmental factors, highlighting the need for continued conservation efforts to preserve these essential marine habitats.
