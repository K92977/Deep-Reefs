Angria Bank
Off the coast of Vijaydurg, Maharashtra, India

Angria Bank is a shallow, submerged atoll located on the continental shelf off the west coast of India, approximately 105 kilometers (65 miles) west of Vijaydurg, Maharashtra. This bank features a platform-type coral reef and derives its name from Kanhoji Angre, a renowned admiral in the Maratha Empire whose name was Anglicized by the British as "Conajee Angria." The atoll’s formation and structure provide a fascinating example of India's offshore coral reefs, which have developed over a submerged plateau on the continental shelf.

Geographically, Angria Bank spans about 40 kilometers (25 miles) from north to south and 15 kilometers (9 miles) from east to west, with an average depth of around 35 meters (115 feet). While certain areas are as shallow as 23 meters (75 feet), the depth can plunge to about 400 meters (1,300 feet) below sea level in other spots. The bottom composition consists mainly of sand, shells, and coral. The bank is steep on all sides, with the surrounding areas marked by deep waters, creating a unique habitat. Angria Bank is located approximately 200 kilometers (120 miles)

north of Adas Bank, another submerged feature off the coast of Goa, highlighting a series of underwater ecosystems along India's western continental shelf.

Marine life at Angria Bank is abundant and diverse. In December 2019, an expedition by the Wildlife Conservation Society of India revealed that the coral reefs on Angria Bank had not experienced bleaching, a common issue in many other reefs. Marine species observed at Angria Bank include the laced moray eel, schooling bannerfish, and the red knob sea star, along with dolphins such as the Indo-Pacific bottlenose and common dolphins in deeper offshore waters. During the eight-day survey, scientists recorded over 150 species of corals and algae, none of which showed visible signs of bleaching, indicating a relatively healthy coral ecosystem that has piqued the interest of conservationists.

As of 2008, Angria Bank could be accessed via a two-hour boat ride from Malvan, Maharashtra. That year, the Maharashtra government announced a ₹50 million initiative, led by Finance Minister Jayant Patil, to explore the site for potential tourism development. Although tourism is still developing, Angria Bank holds considerable promise as a marine tourism destination, given its rich biodiversity and accessible location. The first underwater scientific survey, conducted by the National Institute of Oceanography in 1985, uncovered substantial biodiversity at the site, though the findings have yet to be published.

Bar Reef
Kalpitiya peninsula, Sri Lanka

The Bar Reef, located just off the coast of Sri Lanka's Kalpitiya peninsula, is one of the most biologically diverse coral reef systems in the waters around India and stands as a relatively untouched coral ecosystem in Sri Lanka. This extensive reef complex extends along the coast from the northern end of the Kalpitiya peninsula to the islands that demarcate Portugal Bay from the Gulf of Mannar. Home to 156 species of coral and 283 species of fish, the reef holds high ecological, biological, and aesthetic value, making it one of Sri Lanka's most important marine sanctuaries. In recognition of its unique biodiversity, the Bar Reef was designated a marine sanctuary in 1992, covering an area of 306.7 square kilometers (118.4 square miles).

Historically, the Bar Reef faced numerous threats, both natural and human-induced, due to limited management and protection. Natural factors such as the crown-of-thorns starfish, coastal erosion, and sedimentation posed risks to the reef’s health, while human activities like unmanaged fishing practices and pollution further threatened its stability.

Recognizing the need for intervention, the Asian Development Bank (ADB) initiated a Coastal Resources Management Project (CRMP) in 1999. This project aimed to establish an integrated management approach to protect Sri Lanka’s coastal resources, addressing coastal erosion, pollution, unsustainable fishing, over-exploitation of resources, and poverty among the coastal communities dependent on these ecosystems.

Under this initiative, the Bar Reef was incorporated into a Special Management Area (SAM), which includes the northern Kalpitiya peninsula and islands within Portugal Bay. This designation helps regulate human activities that directly impact the reef's ecosystem, fostering a balance between resource use and environmental conservation. Threats to the Bar Reef's ecosystem have been identified as unsustainable fishing practices, including deep purse seining, which can damage coral structures and deplete fish populations. Pollution from nearby human activities, such as prawn farming and agriculture, also poses ongoing challenges to the health of the reef.

Today, the Bar Reef stands as a significant marine sanctuary, showcasing the value of concerted conservation efforts in maintaining biodiversity and ecological resilience. Despite ongoing challenges, the reef remains a testament to the potential of integrated management and the importance of preserving marine ecosystems for future generations.

Benares Shoals
Peros Banhos atoll, northern Chagos Archipelago

The Benares Shoals, also known as Benares Shoal, is a submerged coral reef located at 5°15′S 071°40′E, in the northern Chagos Archipelago. It sits roughly 6 kilometers (3.7 miles) west-northwest of Île Pierre, the closest island of the Peros Banhos atoll. This isolated reef measures approximately 3 kilometers (2 miles) from east to west, with a width of about 700 meters (2,300 feet) and covers an area of 2 square kilometers (0.77 square miles). Notably, the shoals reach their shallowest point at the western end, with a minimum depth of 4.5 meters (15 feet), making it a distinct feature in this area of the ocean.

The Benares Shoals hold historical significance due to their early survey in 1837 by Commander Robert Moresby of the Indian Navy aboard the HMS *Benares*. Moresby’s work produced the first detailed chart of the reef, contributing valuable navigational information about this region of the Indian Ocean. His exploration and documentation of the shoals were pivotal in the early mapping of the Chagos Archipelago's underwater geography, which was relatively uncharted at that time.


Named in honor of Moresby’s ship, the HMS *Benares*, this reef remains a noteworthy part of the northern Chagos Archipelago’s complex marine ecosystem. The shoals, like other submerged reefs, are important in the local oceanic environment as they provide habitats for diverse marine species and contribute to the overall biodiversity of the region. To this day, the Benares Shoals continue to stand as both a geographical landmark and a historical symbol of early maritime exploration in the Indian Ocean.

Darwin Mounds
Off the northwest coast of Scotland

The Darwin Mounds are a vast field of underwater sand mounds located off Scotland's northwestern coast, discovered in May 1998. This unique habitat, which lies about 1,000 meters (3,300 feet) deep in the North Atlantic, was identified using remote sensing during surveys funded by the oil industry. These surveys were organized by the Atlantic Frontier Environment Network (AFEN), a joint group involving both the oil industry and the UK government. Named after the research vessel, which itself honored Charles Darwin, these mounds cover a vast area approximately 100 square kilometers (39 square miles) and are located about 100 nautical miles (190 kilometers) northwest of Cape Wrath. Hundreds of mounds, each reaching up to 5 meters (16 feet) in height and 100 meters (330 feet) in width, make up the field, with many of them featuring an additional teardrop-shaped tail oriented southwest of the mound. This mound-tail formation is a globally unique characteristic of the Darwin Mounds.

The mounds primarily consist of sand and are thought to be “sand volcanoes,” formed when fluidized sand dewaters,

causing sediment to bubble upward and create a cone-like shape. While sand volcanoes are often found in the Devonian fossil record in the UK and in active seismic areas, tectonic forces are unlikely to be responsible here. Instead, a slumping event on the nearby Wyville-Thomson Ridge is considered a more probable cause. Atop the mounds, cold-water coral species like *Lophelia pertusa* thrive, a noteworthy discovery as this coral typically grows on hard substrates. The Darwin Mounds, however, present a rare case of *Lophelia* colonies growing on sand, offering one of the largest known northern cold-water habitats for coral species and supporting a significant population of the fragile protozoan *Syringammina fragilissima*, or xenophyophore. These organisms, known for their substantial size, grow over 20 centimeters (7.9 inches) and contribute to the area's ecological significance.

The deep-water coral ecosystem on the Darwin Mounds is notably delicate, as these corals are not adapted to withstand the disturbances common in shallow-water coral reefs. Xenophyophores flourish here in high densities, particularly around the mound tails, providing a foundation for diverse marine life. The coral structures attract various marine organisms, including sponges, worms, crustaceans, and mollusks, and serve as habitats for sea urchins, starfish, and crabs. The fish community includes notable species such as blue ling, roundnose grenadier, and the orange roughy, adding further diversity to the ecosystem. To this day, the Darwin Mounds represent a significant natural marine habitat, supporting a broad array of species in an environment that remains remarkably sensitive and largely untouched by human activity.

French Frigate Shoals
Northwestern Hawaiian Islands

The French Frigate Shoals, known in Hawaiian as Kānemilohaʻi, is the largest atoll in the Northwestern Hawaiian Islands and a significant natural and historical site. Named in honor of French explorer Jean-François de Galaup, comte de Lapérouse, who nearly ran his frigates aground there, the shoals span a 20-mile crescent-shaped reef, with twelve sandbars and La Perouse Pinnacle—a volcanic remnant standing 120 feet tall. The total land area of its islands is approximately 61.5 acres, with over 232,000 acres of coral reef surrounding them. Tern Island, the most prominent among the shoals, has an airstrip, seawall, and field station maintained by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service. Located around 487 nautical miles from Honolulu, the French Frigate Shoals serve as an essential breeding ground for turtles, birds, and seals despite their minimal vegetation and freshwater resources, making human habitation challenging. The ecosystem remains under preservation as part of the Hawaiian Islands National Wildlife Refuge and offers a unique setting for research into marine life and coral conservation.

Historically, the shoals played a strategic role during World War II, used by both the Imperial Japanese Navy and the United States military. The Japanese employed the atoll as a staging area during their attack on Hawaii, prompting the U.S. to establish a naval base there soon after. Following the war, the United States Coast Guard set up a LORAN radio navigation station, which operated until the late 20th century. In more recent decades, the shoals' role has shifted primarily toward conservation and scientific study, with Tern Island serving as a research base for oceanographic and biological investigations, including studies on coral health and plastic pollution. This transition reflects the area’s enduring ecological significance, as it remains one of the few habitats supporting numerous endangered marine species and providing essential insights into the health of Pacific coral ecosystems.

The French Frigate Shoals were initially discovered by Lapérouse in 1786, as he navigated his vessels perilously close to its reefs. His expedition mapped the southeastern half of the atoll and documented La Perouse Pinnacle. Over the years, the shoals became a site of frequent shipwrecks, such as the whaling ship *Two Brothers* in 1823, the barque *Gambia* and clipper *Modern Times* in 1859, and the seal-hunting vessel *Mattie E. Dyer* in 1896. During the 19th century, interest arose in mining guano, and in 1859 the United States formally claimed the atoll under the Guano Islands Act, with Lieutenant John M. Brooke taking possession for the U.S. However, the guano deposits proved impractical to exploit. By the early 20th century, the area had shifted toward conservation, with French Frigate Shoals incorporated into the Hawaiian Islands Bird Reservation in 1909.

In contemporary times, the French Frigate Shoals are managed as part of a protected marine reserve, focusing on preservation and research. This protected status has allowed researchers to examine critical environmental issues such as marine plastic pollution and the impact of Pacific storms on coral reefs and marine life. In 2020, one of the islands was officially noted as contaminated with ocean-borne plastic waste. The shoals' enduring ecological and historical importance remains highlighted through ongoing conservation efforts and scientific exploration, cementing their role as a vital sanctuary for marine biodiversity and a living record of Hawaii's natural and human history.

Miri-Sibuti National Park
Sarawak, Malaysia

Miri-Sibuti Coral Reefs National Park, located off the coast of Miri Division in Sarawak, Malaysia, is a prominent protected marine area on the island of Borneo. Known for its excellent diving conditions, the park boasts depths ranging from 7 to 50 meters, with visibility that averages between 10 to 30 meters, depending on the time of year. The most favorable diving period spans from late March through November, with key dive sites such as Anemone Garden, Grouper Patch Reef, Seafan Garden, and the Atago Maru Wreck offering varied underwater experiences. As one of Malaysia’s newest and most extensive marine parks, Miri-Sibuti stands as a significant dive location and an essential offshore national park in Sarawak, drawing divers worldwide to explore its unique marine habitats.

This extensive coral reef park houses a rich diversity of marine species, with coral formations that include soft corals like leather corals, sea whips, Bubble corals, staghorn corals, and the iconic Dendronephthya and Gorgonia sea fans. These corals, combined with numerous sponges such as elephant ear sponges, create a colorful and textured

underwater landscape that serves as a habitat for a variety of marine life. The reef ecosystem supports vibrant fish populations including clownfish, yellow and blueback fusiliers, angelfish, butterflyfish, and larger species like sea perch, lizardfish, barracudas, and the Napoleon wrasse. The presence of reef predators such as sharks adds to the park’s allure for divers, who witness a balanced marine ecosystem where predator and prey interact harmoniously.

Beyond the fish species, the reefs of Miri-Sibuti are home to other fascinating marine creatures. Feather stars, giant clams, and nudibranchs are common sights, contributing to the area’s biodiversity. Sea anemones, which serve as a habitat for clownfish, add to the dynamic reef environment, and sea horses, though often elusive, can occasionally be found by keen-eyed divers. The diversity of marine life forms a captivating underwater ecosystem that supports Sarawak's reputation as a marine biodiversity hotspot, fostering both tourism and conservation awareness.

Miri-Sibuti Coral Reefs National Park has become one of the most significant natural assets in Sarawak. Its establishment not only highlights Malaysia's commitment to marine conservation but also offers an opportunity to protect and study this newly discovered diving region. By attracting international attention, the park plays an essential role in promoting sustainable tourism and marine conservation in Borneo, showcasing the ecological wealth of Malaysia’s offshore waters and inspiring future marine preservation efforts.

Reunion Island's coral reef
Reunion

Réunion Island, located in the Indian Ocean near Madagascar, features a prominent coral reef that spans over 9 kilometers along its western coast, particularly between St. Leu and St. Gilles. This reef varies in width, from 50 meters near St. Gilles in the north to 600 meters in the southern areas, benefiting from deep-sea currents that bring nutrient-rich waters close to the shoreline. These currents contribute to the high biodiversity found within the reef, supporting a vibrant array of marine flora and fauna. This unique reef system, isolated by its distance from the continental shelf, represents a critical habitat in the Indian Ocean, playing a key role in maintaining marine diversity and ecosystem stability in the region.

The coral reef fringing Réunion is densely populated with various hard and soft coral species that provide a home for fish, shellfish, and other marine organisms. These corals, dependent on plankton and algae, have a relatively slow growth rate, with many growing about 10 centimeters per year. Coral reproduction is limited on the reef, partially due

to the smaller number of male coral polyps that release sperm into the water to fertilize eggs from female corals. However, the reef faces growing environmental threats from increased pollution and human activities, which directly affect the water quality and health of coral ecosystems. Pollution can lead to algal blooms and hypoxic conditions, which harm coral growth and contribute to reef degradation. Additionally, global warming poses a severe threat, as even slight increases in water temperature beyond the ideal 23°C to 28°C range can result in coral bleaching and disease outbreaks, further endangering the reef's long-term health.

Cyclones and human-induced disturbances, including urban development along coastal areas, have significantly impacted Réunion's reef. Increased nutrient levels from these activities disrupt the reef’s delicate balance, promoting alterations in the benthic flora and fauna. The coral reef also serves as a natural barrier, protecting the coastline from storms and typhoons. However, areas like St. Paul and l’Etang-Salé, which are less exposed to urban pressures, are relatively stable, while regions such as la Pointe des Aigrettes, la Ravine Blanche, and St-Pierre face heightened vulnerability due to urban encroachment and erosion. The fragile reef areas around Pirogue and St-Pierre Ville are already experiencing irreversible deterioration, as urban expansion and the influx of pollutants disrupt both the physical and biological aspects of the marine environment.

Efforts to conserve Réunion’s coral reefs have been bolstered by the establishment of the Marine Nature Reserve in 2007, which has made strides in raising awareness, enforcing protective regulations, and reducing poaching. Sustainable management of the reef requires assessing its carrying capacity—the threshold beyond which environmental damage becomes irreparable. Carrying capacity takes into account factors such as unplanned development, inadequate waste management, overuse of natural resources, and the impact of growing infrastructure demands. The International Coral Reef Initiative (ICRI), in collaboration with the Global Fund for Coral Reefs (GFCR) and other organizations, has also launched the Coral Reef Breakthrough to safeguard over 125,000 square kilometers of coral reefs by 2030. This initiative aims to combat local stressors, enhance reef protection, restore degraded areas, and secure substantial investments in reef conservation, underscoring the global commitment to preserving coral ecosystems like Réunion's amidst growing environmental challenges.

Somalian Coral Reef
Red Sea to Madagascar

Somalia's coral reefs, located along the country's 3,300-kilometer coastline in the Gulf of Aden and the Indian Ocean, are an essential part of the Western Indian Ocean reef network. Primarily found around northern islands near Zeila and the Bajuni Archipelago in the south, these reefs host a variety of hard and soft corals, sponges, reef fish, and larger species like reef sharks and rays. They are crucial for local fishing communities, providing food and livelihoods.

However, these reefs face threats from climate change, overfishing, and pollution. Rising ocean temperatures have led to coral bleaching, while destructive fishing techniques and untreated waste discharge degrade coral health and reduce fish populations. Conservation efforts are limited but gradually increasing, with international support beginning to encourage sustainable practices and marine protected areas.

Somalia’s coral reefs are essential for biodiversity and coastal protection, offering both ecological and economic benefits. With growing conservation awareness, these reefs could continue to thrive, supporting local communities and marine life alike.
